# linkedin-ads-copy-framework

Use this skill to draft LinkedIn ad copy that stops the scroll by leading
with customer pain and validating it with hard data or social proof.

## Ad Copy Structure (The "4x4" Framework)

A highly converting LinkedIn ad contains 4 distinct elements:

1. **The Scroll-Stopper (Intro):** A contrarian statement, a surprising statistic, or a direct callout of the target persona's biggest unstated pain point.
2. **The Mechanism (Body):** Why they are experiencing the pain (the old way) and the conceptual shift required to solve it (the new way).
3. **The Proof (Validation):** A name-drop, a percentage improvement, or a logical proof point validating the mechanism.
4. **The Direct Ask (CTA):** What exactly happens when they click the button or fill the form.

## Copy Variants by Funnel Stage

### Cold (Top of Funnel)
- Focus: Problem awareness and education.
- Tone: Helpful, authoritative, contrarian.
- Text length: Long-form (3-5 small paragraphs) works exceptionally well here if the story is compelling.
- Example CTA: "See the breakdown," "Read the playbook."

### Warm (Middle of Funnel)
- Focus: Solution evaluation and social proof.
- Tone: Direct, analytical, validating.
- Text length: Medium (2-3 paragraphs). Focus on the "Cost of Inaction."
- Example CTA: "See how [Company] did it," "Calculate your ROI."

### Hot (Bottom of Funnel)
- Focus: Direct conversion, removing friction.
- Tone: Urgent, clear, explicit.
- Text length: Very short (1-2 sentences). Get straight to the point.
- Example CTA: "Book a demo," "Start free trial."

## Formatting Rules

- **Use line breaks generously.** LinkedIn formatting is tight. No paragraph longer than 3 lines.
- **Emojis are acceptable** but use them as bullet points or visual breaks, not as replacements for words.
- **The "See More" cutoff:** The most critical hook must happen in the first 140 characters before the copy is truncated.

## Anti-Patterns
- Opening with "Are you struggling with X?" (Too generic, ignores awareness level).
- Using jargon instead of plain English.
- Hiding the actual offer behind vague "Learn More" CTAs.
- Writing feature lists instead of outcome narratives.
